How We Find Them

Detection Methods &
The Math Behind Them

We can't see exoplanets directly — they're too dim, too far away. Instead, we look for the shadows they cast, the wobbles they cause, and the light they bend.

Transit Photometry — Star Brightness over Time
When a planet crosses its star, it blocks a tiny fraction of light. Kepler measured brightness changes as small as 0.01% — about the shadow of a gnat crossing a car headlight seen from a mile away.
Transit Geometry
Planet Radius from Transit Depth
ΔF / F = (Rp / R★)²
ΔF = flux drop during transit
Rp = planet radius · R★ = stellar radius
Earth/Sun: ΔF ≈ 0.0084% · Jupiter/Sun: ΔF ≈ 1.0%
Orbital Period (Kepler's 3rd Law)
T² = (4π² / GM★) · a³
T = orbital period · a = semi-major axis
M★ = stellar mass · G = gravitational constant
Observe two transits → know the period → know the orbit
Equilibrium Temperature
Teq = T★ · √(R★ / 2a) · (1−A)¼
A = albedo (reflectivity) · a = orbital distance
Earth: Teq ≈ 255 K (−18°C before greenhouse effect)
Transit Depth — Earth vs. Known Planets
Radial Velocity Signal — Stellar Wobble
A planet doesn't orbit its star — both orbit their shared center of mass. As the star wobbles toward and away from us, its light blue-shifts and red-shifts. Jupiter causes a 12 m/s wobble in the Sun. Earth causes just 0.09 m/s.
Doppler Velocity Semi-Amplitude
K = (mp sin i) / √(M★ · a)
K = measured velocity amplitude
mp = planet mass (minimum) · i = orbital inclination
This gives us minimum mass — inclination unknown without a transit
Doppler Wavelength Shift
Δλ / λ = vr / c
vr = radial velocity · c = speed of light · λ = rest wavelength
At 1 m/s: Δλ ≈ 0.002 pm for 500nm light — requires spectrograph precision of 1 part in 3 billion

Radial velocity was the dominant detection method before Kepler. It's biased toward massive, close-in planets — but combined with transits, it gives us both radius and mass, allowing us to compute density and infer composition.

Transmission Spectrum — Atmospheric Fingerprint
During a transit, starlight filters through the planet's atmosphere. Different molecules absorb specific wavelengths. By comparing in-transit vs. out-of-transit spectra, we detect H₂O, CO₂, CH₄, O₃ — potential biosignatures.
Atmospheric Scale Height
H = kBT / (μ · g)
H = scale height (how "puffy" the atmosphere is)
kB = Boltzmann constant · T = temperature · μ = mean molecular weight · g = surface gravity
Larger H → stronger spectral features → easier to detect
Transmission Depth Enhancement
δA = 2 · Rp · N · H / R★²
N ≈ 5–10 scale heights probed · Rp = planet radius
JWST detects features as small as 50 ppm in flux — a revolution in atmospheric science

The James Webb Space Telescope transformed this field. In 2023 it detected CO₂ and SO₂ in the atmosphere of WASP-39b — the first direct detection of photochemistry on another world.
